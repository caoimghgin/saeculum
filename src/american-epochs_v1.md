# American Epochs: A Periodization Framework

A naming convention for significant periods in American history, organized by legitimacy cycles and regime transitions.

---

## The Epochs

| Epoch                   | Years        | Defining Events |
| ----------------------- | ------------ | --------------- |
| The American Ascendancy | 1941–1952    | Lend-Lease (1941), Pearl Harbor (1941)<br>D-Day (1944), Bretton Woods (1944)<br>Hiroshima (1945), Marshall Plan (1948) |
| The Affluent Society    | 1953–1965    | Korean War ends (1953), Brown v. Board (1954)<br>Interstate Highway Act (1956)<br>Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965)<br>Medicare/Medicaid (1965) |
| The Unravelling         | 1966–1979    | Watts riot (1965), Black Power (1966)<br>Detroit/Newark riots (1967), Tet Offensive (1968)<br>MLK and RFK assassinated (1968)<br>Watergate (1972–74), Fall of Saigon (1975)<br>Stagflation (1973–79), Iran hostages (1979) |
| Morning in America      | 1980–1991    | Reagan elected (1980), PATCO strike broken (1981)<br>Tax Reform Act (1986), Berlin Wall falls (1989)<br>Soviet collapse (1991), Gulf War (1991) |
| The End of History      | 1992–2007    | NAFTA (1994), Welfare reform (1996)<br>9/11 (2001), Iraq War (2003)<br>Hurricane Katrina (2005) |
| The Interregnum         | 2008–2015    | Lehman collapse (2008), TARP bailouts (2008)<br>Obama elected (2008), Tea Party wave (2010)<br>Occupy Wall Street (2011) |
| The Reckoning           | 2016–Present | Trump elected (2016), Charlottesville (2017)<br>COVID-19 pandemic (2020), January 6 (2021)<br>Trump re-elected (2024) |

## Justifications

### The American Ascendancy (1941–1952)

*Ascendancy* captures a nation in motion—climbing toward supremacy, the trajectory already unmistakable. The choice of 1941 over 1945 is deliberate: Lend-Lease (March 1941) was the structural pivot. Before a single American soldier died, the United States became the creditor and arsenal of the Allied powers. The British Empire began liquidating its global assets to pay for American materiel. The economic basis of hegemony was established before Pearl Harbor.

Bretton Woods (July 1944) ratified what Lend-Lease created. The conference happened during the war precisely because the financial architecture was already evident. The dollar's supremacy formalized a creditor position already achieved.

The word "ascendancy" rather than "hegemony" preserves the contingency that defined the moment. In 1941, Britain could have fallen, the Soviets could have collapsed, the Pacific could have gone differently. The ascent was real, the destination still uncertain.

Henry Luce—the publishing magnate behind *Time*, *Life*, and *Fortune*—declared "The American Century" in February 1941, one month before Lend-Lease passed. He saw it coming. The isolationists saw it too, which is precisely why they opposed intervention.

---

### The Affluent Society (1953–1965)

John Kenneth Galbraith—Harvard economist and advisor to four presidents—gave the era its name in his 1958 bestseller. The title captured America's self-understanding: the problem of production had been solved; the question was now distribution and quality of life. The consensus was simultaneously political and economic-philosophical. Countercyclical Keynesianism worked. Unions and corporations bargained. Real wages rose with productivity. The GI Bill created a middle class. This was the empirical basis for legitimacy.

1953 marks the moment when the opposition party accepted the New Deal framework. Eisenhower built the Interstate Highway System and preserved Social Security and the regulatory state intact. The Korean War's end removed the last source of wartime uncertainty. What followed was consolidation—the settlement had won.

The endpoint is 1965—the legislative apex. The Voting Rights Act and Medicare/Medicaid passed that summer, the high-water mark of the consensus actually delivering. Yet that same August, Watts burned. The first major crack appeared at the moment of greatest triumph. Everything after was implementation meeting resistance.

---

### The Unravelling (1966–1979)

The name implies something *woven* coming apart—which is precisely accurate. The postwar settlement was an interlocking system: Keynesian economics, Cold War bipartisanship, labor-capital accord, incremental civil rights gains within the existing order. Each thread pulled loose another.

1966 marks the turn. Stokely Carmichael's "Black Power" speech (June) signaled that the civil rights coalition was fracturing; the incremental gains of the consensus no longer satisfied. The same year saw the first major anti-Vietnam protests and the midterm elections that broke the Great Society's congressional majority. The unravelling had begun.

Tet discredited Cold War leadership. Assassinations removed the figures who might have held coalitions together. Watergate proved institutional rot. Stagflation broke Keynesianism. The unravelling was systemic—each failure connected to the others.

1979 closes the period because the next regime began gestating before Reagan took office. Carter's malaise speech (July) and the Iran hostage crisis (November) bookend the exhaustion. More structurally: Paul Volcker's appointment to the Fed (August 1979)—the monetarist who would break inflation by inducing recession—marked the abandonment of full-employment Keynesianism for inflation-targeting. The policy regime changed before the election.

---

### Morning in America (1980–1991)

Using the Reagan campaign's own slogan is pointed. It acknowledges that Reaganism was a successful ideological project—it restored a sense of legitimacy, purpose, and confidence, whatever one thinks of its content. The name also carries irony: mornings eventually end.

The 1980 election was ideological regime change—a fundamental reordering after 48 years of New Deal governance. The questions changed: from "how do we manage capitalism?" to "how do we unleash it?"

1991 closes the period with the Soviet collapse—vindication of the Cold War project—and the Gulf War's demonstration of unchallengeable military supremacy. The Reagan settlement survived Reagan and proved transferable; Bush governed within it. The triumphalism had earned its confidence.

---

### The End of History (1992–2007)

Francis Fukuyama—State Department policy planner and political theorist—gave the era its name in his 1989 essay and 1992 book. His thesis was precise: the ideological question had been settled. Liberal democratic capitalism had no serious competitor. The Washington Consensus would spread globally. The European Union would consolidate. China would liberalize as it marketized. The debate was over. This was believed sincerely, by serious people, for fifteen years.

Clinton's 1992 election ratified that the Democrats had accepted the Reagan settlement's terms. "The era of big government is over." Triangulation, welfare reform, financial deregulation, NAFTA—the opposition party consolidated the new order. Exactly as Eisenhower's acceptance of the New Deal marked the Affluent Society's start.

Why 2007 rather than 2008? The End of History confidence died before the financial crisis. The Iraq occupation had soured. Katrina revealed state incapacity. The housing market peaked in 2006 and subprime cracks appeared throughout 2007. The legitimacy was already failing; 2008 was the corpse hitting the floor.

---

### The Interregnum (2008–2015)

Antonio Gramsci—the Italian Marxist theorist who wrote his prison notebooks in the 1930s—gave us the word: "The crisis consists precisely in the fact that the old is dying and the new cannot be born; in this interregnum a great variety of morbid symptoms appear."

The neoliberal order demonstrably failed—bailouts for banks, foreclosures for homeowners made the class politics undeniable. Yet nothing replaced it. Obama governed the aftermath, stabilizing the crisis but establishing no new regime: stimulus was too small, no structural reform, no new ideological framework. The Tea Party and Occupy Wall Street were mirror-image symptoms of the same legitimacy vacuum, each incapable of founding a new settlement.

2015 marks the emergence from the vacuum. Trump's campaign announcement (June 2015) signaled that a morbid symptom had become a political force. The interregnum was ending—though into what remained unclear.

---

### The Reckoning (2016–Present)

*Reckoning* implies debts coming due, an accounting that can no longer be deferred. The debts are multiple: deindustrialization's social costs (deferred since the 1980s), racial caste contradictions (deferred since the Founding), institutional legitimacy deficits (accumulated since Watergate), imperial overextension (deferred since 2003). The bill arrived.

Trump became the vehicle for this reckoning precisely because he repudiated the bipartisan consensus that had deferred these debts. Where technocratic reformers offered management, he offered disruption; where Sanders channeled the reckoning leftward, Trump captured the larger share of those who had lost faith in institutions entirely. His 2016 victory—against both party establishments, against polling consensus, against explicit elite opposition—proved the interregnum had produced something. Whether this represents realignment, late-stage crisis, or democratic breakdown remains contested.

The endpoint of "Present" acknowledges that we remain inside this period, still too close to see its shape. The Reckoning continues. Whether January 2025 inaugurates a new settlement or intensifies the crisis remains to be seen.

---

## Structural Logic

The seven epochs trace two legitimacy cycles, each following the same arc: **Rise → Apex → Crisis**.

**Cycle One: The New Deal Order (1941–1979)**
- *Rise*: The American Ascendancy—power accumulates, a new order gestates
- *Apex*: The Affluent Society—the settlement achieves hegemony; the opposition accepts its terms
- *Crisis*: The Unravelling—the conditions of legitimacy erode; the center cannot hold

**Cycle Two: The Reagan-Clinton Synthesis (1980–Present)**
- *Rise*: Morning in America—a new ideological order establishes legitimacy on different terms
- *Apex*: The End of History—the settlement achieves hegemony; again, the opposition accepts its terms
- *Crisis*: The Interregnum and The Reckoning—legitimacy collapses; what comes next remains unresolved

The pattern reveals itself: each order is ratified when the opposition party adopts its framework (Eisenhower accepting the New Deal; Clinton accepting Reaganism). Each order dies when its legitimacy conditions fail (stagflation broke Keynesianism; the financial crisis broke neoliberalism). The question hanging over the present is whether we are witnessing a third cycle beginning—or the exhaustion of the pattern itself.
